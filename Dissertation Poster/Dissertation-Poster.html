Introduction

In order to better understand the resistance of a coating to the atmospheric environment AkzoNobel use “exposure testing site”s (ETSs) where coated substrates are left exposed to the atmosphere and then performance is assessed.

Figure 1: Global Exposure testing sites of AkzoNobel

The aim of this project is to compare the environments of AkzoNobel’s ETSs and how they relate to the range of environments globally.

Objectives

  1. Build a relevant climate data set.
  2. Compare the climate of exposure test sites to each other
  3. Compare climate of exposure test sites to global climates documents
  4. Suggest locations for new ETSs that would improve global climate coverage of ETSs

Methods

Vairables used:

  • Temperature: the mean temperature for each month

  • Precipitation: the total ammount of precipitation each month

  • UV Radiation: the mean UV radiation for each month

  • Time of Wetness: the total time that surfaces are wet in a month

  • Frequency of Wetness: the number of times of wet to dry or dry to wet cycle in each month

Hierarchical clustering on principle components and Partition Around Meidiods were utilised to compare ETS to each other. This was carried out for each month of the year. The number of times two locations were clustered together across the year was used as a measure of how distinct each ETS was.

Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) was used to compare the climate of ETSs to global climates. This was used to create a heatmap that shows the global climate coverage of the existing AkzoNobel ETSs.

Finally, three new ETS locations (Lima, Kinshasa and Jakarta) were suggested based on global areas of low similarity and having distinct climates to existing ETSs. These locations were included in DTW analysis to measure the improvement in global climate coverage.

Next Steps

Identifying the optimum number of ETSs based on the effect of each ETS on global climate coverage.

Improve the method of identifying additional locations that will have significant improvement on the global climate coverage by clustering locations of low similarity and identifying low similarity paragons. Include these paragons in comparison to global climates using Dynamic Time Warping.

Conduct real world exposure testing will to validate the suggestions made in this report.

Conclusion

Existing ETSs that are not significantly different and existing ETSs with distinct climates have been identified.

Global areas of high similarity to current ETS and areas that are not covered by existing ETS have been identified. New ETS locations suggested in this report have not significantly improved global climate coverage.
